# Matrix Sediment Standards Derivation Options Paper

## 1.0 Executive Summary

The British Columbia Ministry of Environment has mandated the Science and Standards Technical Advisory Committee (SSTAC) to modernize the province's sediment quality standards. For decades, British Columbia has relied on empirical, co-occurrence-based guidelines. While historically useful, this status quo is overly conservative and scientifically limited, fundamentally failing to account for the site-specific bioavailability of contaminants. This empirical fallacy often results in overly stringent regulatory triggers that do not accurately reflect the true ecological or human health risks at a given site.

To address these limitations, the SSTAC evaluated transitioning to a modernized, multi-pathway mechanistic framework. By adopting advanced models such as Equilibrium Partitioning (EqP) for direct ecological contact, Biota-Sediment Accumulation Factors (BSAF) for food web transfer, and localized consumption modifiers for human health, the province can derive sediment standards rooted in predictive thermodynamic science. 

Based on rigorous assessment against criteria of scientific defensibility, regulatory feasibility, and Indigenous integration, this paper officially recommends **Option 3: The Tiered Hybrid Framework**. This approach strategically balances the practical need for rapid screening with the necessity for highly localized, mechanistic rigor. 

Ultimately, the Tiered Hybrid Framework ensures that British Columbia remains at the forefront of environmental protection, providing a scientifically robust, feasible, and equitable system that actively integrates Indigenous traditional knowledge and protects subsistence harvesting rights.


## 2.0 Context & Problem Statement

The modernization of sediment quality standards in British Columbia requires a fundamental shift from historical evaluation paradigms toward a more nuanced, site-specific understanding of contaminant behavior. Historically, regulatory frameworks have relied heavily on empirical, co-occurrence-based guidelines. While these approaches established a necessary baseline for environmental protection, they inherently fail to capture the complex physicochemical dynamics that dictate actual exposure and risk within aquatic ecosystems.

### The Empirical Fallacy
The primary limitation of traditional empirical guidelines is the assumption that the "total recoverable" concentration of a contaminant directly correlates with its toxicity. This correlative approach leads to significant regulatory and ecological shortcomings:
- **Bioavailability Ignored:** Empirical methods fail to distinguish between inert, bound contaminants and the biologically active fraction available for uptake by organisms. 
- **Overly Conservative Remediation:** Assuming 100% bioavailability often results in highly conservative standards that trigger costly, unnecessary remediation of sediments where contaminants are tightly bound and pose no genuine toxicological threat.
- **False Negatives in Sensitive Environments:** Conversely, in environments with low binding capacity, empirical standards may underestimate risk, leading to false negatives that fail to protect sensitive benthic or human receptors.

### The Need for a Mechanistic Matrix
To resolve the limitations of the empirical fallacy, British Columbia must transition to a mechanistic, multi-pathway matrix framework. A mechanistic approach relies on thermodynamic and biological principles to predict the actual freely dissolved, bioavailable concentration of a contaminant.
- **Site-Specific Parameters:** By integrating normalizing factors—such as Total Organic Carbon (TOC) for non-ionic organic compounds and Acid Volatile Sulfides (AVS) for divalent metals—regulators can accurately assess site-specific toxicity.
- **Holistic Protection:** A matrix framework ensures that standards are dynamically tailored to protect both ecological receptors (benthic invertebrates, higher-trophic aquatic life) and human health, rather than relying on a static, one-size-fits-all number.

### Phase 2 Scope and Objectives
The current phase of the Sediment Science Technical Advisory Committee (SSTAC) work is focused exclusively on the methodological derivation options that will power this new matrix. 
- **Methodological Evaluation:** The scope encompasses the rigorous evaluation of scientific frameworks (e.g., Equilibrium Partitioning, Biota-Sediment Accumulation Factors) to address the four primary derivation pathways: Ecological Direct Contact, Ecological Food Web, Human Health Direct Contact, and Human Health Food Web.
- **Foundation for Implementation:** This options paper will not prescribe final numeric standards. Rather, it delivers the foundational methodological architecture and evaluation criteria required by the BC Ministry of Environment to mandate a modernized, defensible regulatory framework in subsequent phases of implementation.


## 3.0 Jurisdictional Scan & Scientific Frameworks

### 3.1 Global Methodological Shifts
Leading international regulatory bodies—most notably the United States Environmental Protection Agency (US EPA) and the Australian and New Zealand Guidelines for Fresh and Marine Water Quality (ANZG)—have successfully navigated the transition away from strictly empirical, correlative sediment guidelines. By adopting mechanistic models rooted in equilibrium partitioning and bioavailability, these jurisdictions have established frameworks that are both more scientifically robust and practically effective at diagnosing site-specific toxicity. This paradigm shift provides a proven roadmap for British Columbia's modernization efforts.

### 3.2 Ecological Direct Contact (EqP & AVS)
The Ecological Direct Contact pathway focuses on the protection of benthic macroinvertebrates that live within or directly upon the sediment matrix. To accurately assess toxicity, the framework relies on Equilibrium Partitioning (EqP) theory for non-ionic organic compounds and Acid Volatile Sulfide (AVS) normalization for divalent metals. 
- **Organic Compounds:** Toxicity is governed by the concentration of the contaminant freely dissolved in the interstitial porewater, which is highly dependent on the sediment's Total Organic Carbon (TOC) fraction.
- **Metals:** The toxicity of cationic metals (e.g., Cd, Cu, Pb, Ni, Zn, Ag) is mitigated by their binding affinity to sulfide in anaerobic sediments.

For full mathematical derivations, see Technical Appendix A: EqP & AVS Framework.

### 3.3 Ecological Food Web (BSAF)
The Ecological Food Web pathway is designed to protect higher-trophic-level aquatic life and piscivorous wildlife from bioaccumulative contaminants (e.g., PCBs, dioxins, methylmercury). This framework utilizes Biota-Sediment Accumulation Factors (BSAF) to back-calculate protective sediment concentrations from established fish or wildlife tissue thresholds. Following methodologies pioneered by the California State Water Resources Control Board (SWRCB), this approach explicitly accounts for site-specific lipid fractions in target species and the organic carbon content of the sediment, enabling a dynamic assessment of trophic transfer risks.

For full mathematical derivations, see Technical Appendix B: BSAF Framework.

### 3.4 Human Health Pathways
Protecting human populations requires assessing both direct contact with contaminated sediments and the consumption of aquatic organisms that have bioaccumulated sediment-bound toxicants. Standard deterministic risk assessment equations are heavily modified for aquatic and estuarine environments to reflect high-frequency contact scenarios. 
- **Incidental Ingestion and Dermal Contact:** Variables are adjusted to account for wetted-sediment adherence factors and localized recreational or occupational exposure frequencies.
- **Traditional Food Consumption:** Incorporating elevated, highly specific ingestion rates of local fish and shellfish ensures the protection of high-exposure subpopulations, notably Indigenous communities relying on traditional aquatic diets.

For full mathematical derivations, see Technical Appendix C: Human Health Pathways.


## 4.0 Evaluation Criteria

To ensure a rigorous, objective, and transparent decision-making process, the Technical Working Group (TWG) will assess all proposed regulatory frameworks against a standardized rubric. This multi-dimensional evaluation ensures that any modernized approach balances scientific rigor with practical implementation constraints.

### 4.1 Scientific Defensibility
The foundational criterion for any proposed matrix option is its alignment with modern, peer-reviewed aquatic toxicology and environmental chemistry. 
- Does the framework accurately capture the thermodynamic principles of bioavailability (e.g., equilibrium partitioning, sulfide binding)? 
- Does it correctly model bioaccumulation dynamics (e.g., BSAF) for higher trophic levels, rather than relying on flawed empirical correlations?

### 4.2 Regulatory & Analytical Feasibility
A scientifically perfect model is useless if it cannot be practically enforced or measured by industry and regulators.
- **Analytical Capacity:** Can commercial environmental laboratories reliably and cost-effectively measure the required input parameters (e.g., Total Organic Carbon [TOC], Acid Volatile Sulfide [AVS]) at the necessary detection limits?
- **Regulatory Burden:** Does the framework impose an unmanageable regulatory burden on proponents for low-risk, routine dredging or remediation sites?

### 4.3 Indigenous Integration
In alignment with the province's commitment to reconciliation and the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (DRIPA), the framework must accommodate and protect Indigenous values.
- Does the framework possess the flexibility to incorporate site-specific traditional use modifiers (e.g., localized, high-frequency aquatic food consumption rates)?
- Can Indigenous knowledge be effectively interwoven with the mechanistic science (Two-Eyed Seeing) during the risk assessment process?


## 5.0 Proposed Matrix Implementation Options

Based on the jurisdictional scan and the established evaluation criteria, the following three implementation options are presented for consideration by the BC Ministry of Environment.

### Option 1: Status Quo (Empirical Guidelines)
This option maintains the current framework, relying entirely on static, co-occurrence-based empirical guidelines for "total recoverable" contaminants.
- **Assessment:** While highly feasible due to established laboratory practices, this option critically fails the *Scientific Defensibility* criterion by ignoring bioavailability. Furthermore, its static nature fails the *Indigenous Integration* criterion, as it lacks the mechanical flexibility to adjust for site-specific traditional consumption rates.

### Option 2: Universal Mechanistic Adoption
This option mandates the strict, universal application of full mechanistic modeling (EqP, AVS, and BSAF frameworks) across all contaminated aquatic sites in the province, regardless of size or risk profile.
- **Assessment:** This option maximizes *Scientific Defensibility* and enables dynamic *Indigenous Integration*. However, it catastrophically fails the *Regulatory & Analytical Feasibility* criterion. Mandating complex, expensive AVS and detailed lipid fraction analyses for every minor, low-risk project would paralyze the regulatory review process and overwhelm commercial laboratory capacity.

### Option 3: Tiered Hybrid Framework (Recommended)
This option implements a pragmatic, two-tiered approach that harmonizes empirical screening with mechanistic precision.
- **Tier 1 (Screening):** Utilizes highly conservative empirical values as a rapid, cost-effective screening tool for low-risk sites.
- **Tier 2 (Mechanistic Matrix):** If a site fails the Tier 1 screen, or if it intersects with specific Indigenous harvesting rights or sensitive ecological zones, it triggers a mandatory site-specific mechanistic derivation (EqP, BSAF, or Human Health modifiers).
- **Assessment:** This is the recommended approach. It perfectly balances all three criteria: preserving regulatory *Feasibility* for routine sites, upholding rigorous *Scientific Defensibility* for complex or highly contaminated areas, and providing the localized mechanical flexibility necessary for genuine *Indigenous Integration*.


## 6.0 Indigenous Knowledge Integration

A modernized sediment quality framework must actively support the province's commitment to reconciliation and align with the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act (DRIPA). The recommended tiered matrix framework achieves this by explicitly integrating Indigenous rights and traditional knowledge into the core mechanical derivations of sediment standards.

### 6.1 Two-Eyed Seeing Approach
The framework is structurally guided by the principle of "Two-Eyed Seeing" (Etuaptmumk). It weaves together the strengths of Western mechanistic science (predictive modeling of bioavailability and trophic transfer) with Indigenous traditional knowledge (localized ecological baselines, harvesting practices, and culturally significant receptor identification). This conceptual weaving ensures that the rigid mathematical models are grounded by localized, lived realities.

### 6.2 Quantitative Integration (Consumption Modifiers)
The primary mechanism for integrating Indigenous rights within the matrix framework occurs during the Tier 2 Human Health pathway derivations. Standard deterministic risk assessments often rely on generalized recreational or general population consumption rates (e.g., 32 g/day of aquatic foods). To protect subsistence harvesting and traditional diets, the framework mechanically inserts localized, high-volume consumption modifiers (e.g., 142 g/day) directly into the exposure equations. This quantitative adjustment ensures that derived sediment standards are sufficiently stringent to protect the health of Indigenous communities consuming traditional aquatic diets.

### 6.3 Collaborative Governance
The integration of traditional knowledge cannot occur in isolation. The tiered matrix framework necessitates active, collaborative governance. During any Tier 2 site-specific assessment that intersects with traditional territories or harvesting rights, Indigenous participation is essential for determining the appropriate localized input parameters (e.g., identifying target species for BSAF modeling, establishing site-specific exposure frequencies).


## 7.0 Recommendations & Next Steps

This Options Paper presents the foundational methodologies and regulatory pathways necessary for modernizing British Columbia's sediment quality standards. The selection of the final implementation framework requires structured evaluation and consensus from key stakeholders.

### 7.1 TWG Review Process
The Technical Working Group (TWG) will utilize the interactive Matrix Options Dashboard to explore the proposed mathematical derivations and regulatory options. The dashboard's Review Portal will facilitate the collection of structured polling data and open-ended feedback, allowing the TWG to systematically evaluate the frameworks against the established rubric (Scientific Defensibility, Feasibility, and Indigenous Integration).

### 7.2 Final Selection
Following the conclusion of the TWG review period, the aggregated feedback will be analyzed to formalize a consensus recommendation. This final, unified recommendation will be formally submitted to the BC Ministry of Environment, establishing the chosen regulatory framework (e.g., the Tiered Hybrid Approach).

### 7.3 Phase 3 Roadmap
Upon acceptance of the recommended framework by the province, the project will transition into Phase 3. The targeted timeline for this regulatory phase is as follows:
- **Q1-Q2 2027: Regulatory Drafting:** Developing the formal legal language, technical guidance documents, and standardized laboratory protocols required for the new matrix derivations.
- **Q3 2027: Public & Stakeholder Consultation:** Releasing the draft regulatory framework for broad public comment, industry review, and formal First Nations consultation.
- **Q4 2027 - Q1 2028: Revisions and Finalization:** Incorporating consultation feedback and conducting final internal legal and scientific reviews.
- **Q2 2028: Policy Implementation:** Official promulgation of the modernized Matrix Sediment Standards into provincial regulation.
