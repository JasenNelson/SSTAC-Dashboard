﻿Advanced Mathematical Frameworks for Sediment-Specific Human Health Risk Assessments: Back-Calculations, Indigenous Consumption Adjustments, and Wetted Matrix Dermal Exposures
Introduction to Matrix-Specific Environmental Risk Modeling
The derivation of human health risk criteria for aquatic and benthic environments requires a complex mathematical departure from standard deterministic risk assessment methodologies. Historically, environmental health frameworks have relied heavily upon standard risk equations calibrated primarily for terrestrial dry soils, ambient atmospheric air quality, and generalized, central-tendency groundwater ingestion rates. However, when assessing contaminated sediments, these baseline deterministic models become mathematically and physically inadequate. They require necessary and highly specific structural transformations to account for the trophic transfer of hydrophobic contaminants in complex aquatic food webs, the localized and statistically specific human dietary consumption patterns of specialized populations, and the unique fluid mechanics and physical properties of wetted sediment matrices interacting with the human epidermal boundary.
The necessity for these specific mathematical deviations arises from the fundamental physical state of sediment and its distinct operational pathways to human receptors. Unlike dry soil, which primarily exposes receptors through direct incidental ingestion or dust inhalation, contaminated aquatic sediment interacts with human physiology through highly specialized indirect and direct pathways. Indirectly, sediment functions as a foundational repository for bioaccumulative toxicants, requiring regulatory frameworks to translate human toxicological limits backward through complex ecological food webs to establish permissible sediment concentrations. Directly, human dermal contact with submerged or intertidal sediment is governed by hydrostatic forces, particulate suspension dynamics, and capillary moisture parameters that render dry soil adherence formulas mathematically obsolete and physically invalid. Furthermore, standard consumption parameter defaults utilized in generalized risk equations drastically fail to capture the exposure realities of populations relying heavily on subsistence aquatic foraging.
This comprehensive technical report extracts, dissects, and maps the exact mathematical adjustments required to convert baseline deterministic health risk equations into specialized, sediment-specific formulations. The analytical structure is divided into three primary extraction domains, representing distinct structural modifications utilized across major jurisdictional regulatory frameworks. First, the report isolates and extracts the bioaccumulation back-calculation formulas utilized by the California State Water Resources Control Board (SWRCB) and the Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA). These equations are utilized to derive safe sediment threshold concentrations indirectly from target fish tissue limits. Second, the analysis extracts and dissects the specific algebraic equations and isolated numerical parameters formulated in the Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation (ACFN) Water Quality Criteria for Indigenous Use (WQCIU) report, demonstrating how precise, high-end traditional food ingestion rates mathematically override default deterministic variables. Third, the report isolates the direct dermal contact equations from Health Canada’s Supplemental Guidance on Human Health Risk Assessment of Contaminated Sediments, explicitly extracting the "wetted sediment" dermal adherence rate variables while strictly and intentionally excluding generic terrestrial dry soil physical parameters.
Part I: Bioaccumulation Back-Calculation Equations and the Derivation of Sediment Standards

The mathematical linkage between contaminated bedded sediments and human health risk is predominantly indirect, mediated continuously by the ecological architecture of aquatic food webs. Human exposure in these specific environmental scenarios is primarily driven by the consumption of higher-trophic-level aquatic organisms, such as piscivorous fish, which have systemically bioaccumulated sediment-bound organic and inorganic contaminants over their lifespans. To establish enforceable regulatory standards and remedial action objectives for the benthic sediment matrix itself, risk assessors must implement a rigorous back-calculation methodology.1 This inverse mathematical approach requires first calculating a scientifically defensible safe threshold for the consumed biological tissue, and subsequently running a calibrated thermodynamic bioaccumulation model in reverse to determine the maximum permissible chemical concentration within the abiotic sediment matrix that will maintain those safe tissue levels.2
The Theoretical Foundation of Fish Contaminant Goals (FCGs)
The operational foundation of the back-calculation framework relies entirely upon establishing precise, safe target tissue concentrations. Within the California jurisdictional framework, these are defined by the Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA) as Fish Contaminant Goals (FCGs) and Advisory Tissue Levels (ATLs).3 These mathematically derived goals represent the exact target biological tissue concentration at which human consumption exposure equates directly to the established physiological toxicological reference level for a given chemical, scaled to specific consumption frequencies.3
The mathematical structure and parametric composition of the FCG equations vary fundamentally depending on the established toxicological profile of the specific contaminant. Risk assessment frameworks bifurcate the mathematical approach based on whether the substance exhibits stochastic carcinogenic effects, deterministic non-carcinogenic systemic toxicity, or acts dually as a non-carcinogenic essential physiological nutrient that presents toxicity only at elevated exposure thresholds.6

Part II: Mathematical Integration of Indigenous Traditional Dietary Parameters
Standard deterministic risk equations consistently rely on central tendency exposure variables that are formulated mathematically for massive, generalized regional populations. However, when these rigid, default parametric models are applied indiscriminately to specific geographic jurisdictions heavily reliant on traditional subsistence foraging and harvesting—such as First Nations territories—these default variables systematically and mathematically dilute the calculated human health risk.15 Because generalized dietary input variables fail to represent the massive volumetric intake of locally harvested wild foods, the resulting framework produces artificially permissive, highly dangerous sediment and aquatic water quality regulatory thresholds that entirely fail to protect the target population.
The Athabasca Chipewyan First Nation (ACFN) Water Quality Criteria for Indigenous Use (WQCIU) framework provides the explicit, scientifically rigorous algebraic modifications required to override these generalized population inputs.16 Specifically, the WQCIU framework extracts precise, statistically robust, high-end traditional dietary ingestion parameters from community surveys and surgically integrates them into the mathematical denominators of the foundational health risk equations.16 Furthermore, to handle the vast uncertainty inherent in sparse environmental monitoring data, the computational analysis behind the WQCIU framework incorporates advanced statistical treatments. For example, for datasets characterized by high percentages of censored data (results below laboratory detection limits), the computational framework employs the Kaplan-Meier method to calculate reliable summary statistics.16 In highly specific cases where the dataset exceeds 50 independent samples and the censoring rate ranges between 50% and 80%, the framework utilizes robust regression on order statistics (robust ROS) methods to accurately mathematically estimate the 5th, 50th, and 95th percentiles of exposure concentrations before they are input into the broader risk equations.16
