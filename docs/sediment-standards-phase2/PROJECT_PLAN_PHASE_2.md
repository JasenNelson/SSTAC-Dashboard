## 1. Project Overview

### Project Purpose

The purpose of Phase 2 of the Sediment Standards Project is to develop a modern scientific framework
for updating BC's Contaminated Sites Regulation (CSR) Schedule 3.4 Generic Numerical Sediment
Standards, integrating best-available science to protect aquatic ecosystems and the communities that
depend on them.

### Problem Statement

BC's current generic sediment standards framework is pathway-limited, focusing primarily on benthic
invertebrate protection through direct contact. This creates systemic gaps for bioaccumulative
substances, higher trophic ecological receptors, and human health (via direct contact and dietary
exposure). Consequently, the framework fails to align with modern risk assessment best practices,
complicates the assessment of high-risk sites, and necessitates a reliance on site-specific
assessments.

### Vision Statement

Establish a fully integrated sediment standards framework that protects ecological and human health
across all exposure routes. Modernize Schedule 3.4 to include three parts:

- Part 1: Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards to protect human and ecological health, including four
  receptor-pathways:
  - Protect Human Health - Direct Exposure to Sediment Contaminants;
  - Protect Human Health - Exposure to Sediment Contaminants in Food;
  - Protect Ecological Health - Direct Exposure to Sediment Contaminants; and
  - Protect Ecological Health - Exposure to Sediment Contaminants in Food.
- Part 2: Generic Numerical Sediment Standards to protect human health; and
- Part 3: Generic Numerical Sediment Standards to protect ecological health.

The Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards represent a refined approach for priority substances that pose
a threat to human and ecological health, and thus require a high duty of care, determined by
considering key factors such as biomagnification, toxicity, persistence, and local occurrence. The
Generic Numerical Sediment Standards are values adopted from other jurisdictions, since it is not
feasible to develop Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards for all substances due to limitations in
available information. This modern multi-part approach with matrix and generic standards creates a
defensible, scalable, and adaptable foundation for the contaminated sites regulatory framework.

### Project Objectives

- Develop a modern sediment standard framework to protect aquatic ecosystems and the human communities
  that depend on them, including:
  - CSR Schedule 3.4 Part 1 Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards to protect human and ecological health
    from direct exposure and food-based exposure to contaminants. These require a comprehensive set of
    equations for each receptor-pathway group that are needed to derive these standards;
  - CSR Schedule 3.4 Part 2 Generic Numerical Sediment Standards to protect human health. These
    require a procedure for adopting values from other jurisdictions (e.g., United States, Australia,
    New Zealand) which have different protection levels, species and other factors from Canadian
    jurisdictions requiring careful consideration, which can leverage work being done on Director's
    Interim Standards; and
  - CSR Schedule 3.4 Part 3 Generic Numerical Sediment Standards to protect ecological health. These
    require the same procedure as Part 2 above.
- Given that Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards require specific information (e.g., biomagnification,
  toxicity, persistence, and local occurrence) and considerable effort, we are developing a substance
  prioritization framework to identify priority substances requiring standards based on their
  potential to cause unacceptable risk in BC's aquatic receiving environments;
- BC Aquatic Database to be developed by the Ministry of Environment and Parks (ENV) based on
  documents submitted to the Site Remediation Program and potentially shared data sets. This is a
  large undertaking requiring significant time and resources in which the partnership with the Science
  Advisory Board for Contaminated Sites (SABCS) will play a key role. This database is critical for
  multiple internal uses, including:
  - Establish concentration ranges for substances in sediments across BC, which could be used to
    develop provincial and regional background concentrations. Understanding these concentration
    ranges are needed to ensure standards are not below natural background concentrations;
  - Identify occurrence of substances in BC sediments to understand prevalence of non-scheduled
    substances, and substances with concentrations above current CSR generic numerical standards and
    screening levels from other jurisdictions; and
  - Determine which substances in BC sediments require site-specific risk-based assessment and whether
    and how bioavailability adjustment played a role in meeting CSR risk-based standards.
- Compile a comprehensive set of Policy-ready input parameters required to calculate the Matrix
  Numerical Sediment Standards in Phase 3 of the Sediment Standards Project. These input parameters
  will include Toxicity Reference Values for protection of human and ecological health, which is a
  considerable undertaking. Other examples of types of input parameters include but are not limited to
  sediment grain size thresholds, receptor characteristics, exposure assumptions, water lot use
  classifications. Protocol 28 provides input parameters for other media with CSR standards.
- The final deliverable for the Sediment Standards Project in Phase 2 is the Matrix Options Paper,
  which describes how the above objectives were completed including research findings, progress made,
  options considered, and detailed rationale supporting recommendations. The Matrix Options Paper will
  provide an engagement summary documenting the ongoing collaborative partnership between ENV and the
  SABCS, including the invaluable contributions by members of the Science and Standards Technical
  Advisory Committee (SSTAC) and the Technical Working Group (TWG).

## 2. Project Scope

### In Scope

- First Quarter (ongoing): Initiate BC Aquatic Database development early since this is a large,
  complex task and requires fully utilizing the TWG support. Data compilation includes:
  - 1) Substance occurrence data in BC to support prioritization;
  - 2) Sediment concentration data to identify natural background conditions and support development
    of provincial and regional background concentrations. This is important for adjusting preliminary
    toxicity-based standards, so the approved standards are not below natural background
    concentrations; and
  - 3) Sediment concentration data to identify the prevalence of concentrations above the current CSR
    Schedule 3.4 Generic Numerical Sediment Standards. This will also help identify where CSR
    risk-based standards are used on a site-specific basis and the importance of bioavailability
    adjustments to account for environmental conditions.
- First Quarter (Months 1-3): Identify options for equations to derive Matrix Numerical Sediment
  Standards for each of the four receptor-pathway combinations.
- First Quarter (ongoing): Initiate data compilation of policy-ready input parameters required for
  calculating preliminary toxicity-based standards (in Phase 3). It is a large undertaking to compile
  this comprehensive set of information, so we need to start early and optimally leverage TWG support
  to assist with compilation and review of values, information and references.
- Second and Third Quarters (Months 4-9): Initiate phased research on key topics to support the
  options analysis process, including bioavailability (Months 4-6), bioaccumulation (Months 5-7),
  substance classification (Months 6-8), and matrix framework design (Months 7-9).
- Third Quarter (Months 7-9): Develop a substance prioritization framework for identifying which
  substances should be considered for Matrix Numerical Sediment Standards.
- Third Quarter (Months 7-9): Develop a procedure for adopting values for Generic Numerical Sediment
  Standards.
- Final Quarter (Months 10-12): Draft the Matrix Options Paper to summarize the collaborative work
  completed in 2026 associated with this project.
- Regular briefings with updates, progress and next steps for ENV, the SABCS and the SSTAC.
- Coordination of the TWG with a sub-group "core" that supports with research, drafting, and editing
  tasks, while the full TWG supports by sharing information and reviewing and providing feedback on
  draft materials.

### Out of Scope

- Final numeric standards
- Regulatory amendments
- Supporting Technical Guidance or Protocol
- Complete sediment database

## Appendix A - Executive Elevator Pitch (for leadership briefing)

*Plain-language briefing for the Project Sponsor to share with executive leadership. Does not reference
internal tooling.*

### 30-second version

British Columbia's sediment standards are decades behind the science. They protect bottom-dwelling
organisms from direct contact, but they miss the contaminants that build up the food chain into fish,
wildlife, and the people who eat them - which is exactly where our highest-risk and highest-profile
sites sit. The Sediment Standards Project modernizes the regulation with a defensible, multi-part
framework that protects both ecological and human health across every exposure route. The science is
being done at minimal cost to government by a volunteer board of BC's leading contaminated-sites
experts, and it delivers a ready-to-consider package to ENV - who remains the sole decision-maker.

### One-minute version (with SABCS / SSTAC background)

**The problem.** BC's generic numerical sediment standards (CSR Schedule 3.4) are pathway-limited -
built mainly to protect benthic invertebrates from direct contact. They do not account for substances
that bioaccumulate up the food web into higher organisms and people, nor for human health through
direct contact or diet. That gap forces costly, inconsistent site-specific assessments and makes our
largest sites harder to manage and defend.

**The fix.** Phase 2 builds directly on Phase 1 (2025), when this same ENV-SABCS partnership - through
an inclusive, collaborative process with significant volunteer expert support - developed and
stress-tested the framework for modernizing sediment standards that now guides our foundational
research. On that foundation, Phase 2 develops a modern, multi-part Schedule 3.4: matrix standards for
priority substances that pose the greatest threat (set by considering biomagnification, toxicity,
persistence, and local occurrence), and generic standards adopted from peer jurisdictions for
everything else. The result is a scalable, defensible foundation that protects ecological and human
health - including Indigenous food-based exposure pathways - across all routes.

**Who is doing it, and the cost.** This work is led by the Science and Standards Technical Advisory
Committee (SSTAC), established by the Science Advisory Board for Contaminated Sites (SABCS) in
partnership with ENV, and co-chaired by an ENV Senior Risk Assessment Officer and a SABCS scientist.
It draws on a 39-plus-member volunteer Technical Working Group spanning industry, consulting, academia,
government, and Indigenous organizations. SABCS solely funded Phase 1, and Phase 2 is scoped to run
within SABCS's confirmed baseline budget - a highly cost-effective, volunteer-driven model that
delivers world-class science at minimal cost to the Province.

**The ask and the boundary.** Phase 2 delivers a Matrix Options Paper - a scientifically defensible
recommendation package, not a regulatory amendment. ENV remains the sole legal decision-maker and will
follow its own policy, consultation, and approval processes before any change to the CSR. We are asking
executive leadership to support continued ENV participation in this partnership, which modernizes a
core piece of BC's contaminated-sites framework using existing authority and largely external,
volunteer expertise.
